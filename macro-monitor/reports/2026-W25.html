Executive Summary§ 1
The week's pivotal event has landed: at Chair Kevin Warsh's debut meeting (17 June) the Federal Reserve held at 3.50–3.75% but flipped to a hawkish stance — its new projections point to a 2026 rate hike rather than the cuts markets had assumed. Investors agree: futures now price an October hike, with cuts fully removed. What the Data Is Saying§ 3

Inflation — hot headline, cooling core

The energy shock is inflating top-line prices across the board: CPI +4.2% y/y (energy +23.5% y/y) and PPI +6.5% y/y, the hottest since late 2022. Beneath the surface, the Fed-relevant gauges are easing — core CPI +0.2% m/m and core PPI +0.4% m/m, both below expectations and the softest in months. The read: a supply-led, commodity move — not broad demand pressure. Next test: May PCE on 25 June.

Energy lifts the headline figures, but core (ex food & energy) sits well below — the signature of a price shock, not a spiral.

Labour & growth — firm today, softening at the edges

Hiring is solid on current data: May payrolls beat at +172K, unemployment steady at 4.3%, and wage growth contained (AHE +3.4% y/y, below inflation) — the equity-friendlier "strong jobs without hot wages" mix. But the forward indicators keep cooling together: the four-week average of jobless claims has risen to 223K, a three-month high; the JOLTS hiring rate has slipped to 3.2%; and ISM employment sits below 50 in both manufacturing and services. An early-warning alignment — not yet a confirmed turn, but drifting the wrong way. Growth, meanwhile, is a tale of two readings: May retail sales beat at +0.9% m/m with a firm core, yet the gain was largely higher gasoline prices — in real (inflation-adjusted) terms spending was roughly flat to slightly negative, and factory output was unchanged. Nominal demand is holding up; real volumes are soft.

Housing — leading gauge turned soft, caution warranted

The forward signal weakened sharply: May housing starts collapsed −15.4% to a 1.177M annual pace — the lowest since 2020 and a large miss — though building permits, the more leading gauge, held far better at 1.413M. Builders are holding authorizations but not breaking ground, with the 30-yr mortgage stuck near 6.44% and a now hike-biased Fed offering no relief. Lagging demand is still firm (existing-home sales 4.17M, best since December, reflecting closings signed earlier at lower rates), but that looks backward. May new-home sales (24 Jun) are the next tell.

Policy & markets — the hawkish turn is now confirmed

The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% on 17 June, but Chair Warsh's debut projections were the story: the 2026 median "dot" moved up to roughly 3.8% — implying a hike rather than the cut pencilled in March — with about half of officials now penciling higher rates and the core-inflation forecast raised. The market firmed, rather than faded, that path: futures fully price an October hike and a year-end rate near 4.0%, with cuts gone. Crucially the repricing has been orderly, not stressed: the yield curve flattened but stays positively sloped (10-yr minus 2-yr at +28bp, no recession signal), high-yield credit spreads actually tightened to 263bp, and equity volatility fell back to ~16. Abroad, the BoE held at 3.75% with a hawkish 7-2 split (two members voted to hike), and the ECB, at a 2.25% deposit rate, is boxed in by stagflation — euro-area inflation +3.1% y/y against a Q1 GDP contraction (−0.2%). Developed-market policy has aligned to the hawkish side.
