Executive Summary§ 1
The U.S. economy enters mid-July with the spring energy shock fully unwound and disinflation back in control. June sealed a dual dovish surprise — core CPI was flat on the month (0.0% m/m), annual core eased to +2.6% y/y, and producer prices actually fell (PPI −0.3% m/m). The labour market is now the swing factor: June payrolls badly missed at +57K with −74K of prior-month revisions, yet weekly jobless claims fell to 208K, the fewest in ten weeks — a turn signalled by the monthly data but, so far, contradicted by the higher-frequency claims. Growth is still expanding (June retail control-group sales +0.5%; both ISM surveys above 50), consumer sentiment is rebounding (UMich 54.4), long-run inflation expectations are anchored at 3.3%, and financial conditions are loose and orderly. Attention now turns to the 28–29 July FOMC — Chair Kevin Warsh's decision on whether to open the door to a September cut.

What the Data Is Saying§ 3

Inflation — the energy shock fully reverses

June delivered a clean dovish surprise on both price gauges. Core CPI was flat (0.0% m/m) — the first flat reading since 2023 — pulling annual core down to +2.6% y/y, its closest to the Fed's 2% target in years, with shelter posting its smallest monthly gain since 2021. Producer prices fell −0.3% m/m as final-demand goods deflated −1.4%. The March–June oil spike (Iran / Strait of Hormuz) has now washed completely out of both series; headline CPI (+3.5% y/y) and PPI (+5.5% y/y) stay elevated only on base effects, while core PPI eased to about +4.7%. The last sticky reading is core PCE at 3.4% y/y (May); the June PCE print (~29–31 July) is the marquee test before the Fed.

The energy spike has washed out of the monthly data. Year-on-year headlines stay elevated on base effects, but core measures — the Fed's focus — are easing back toward target.

Labour & growth — a hiring turn, but claims push back

The monthly labour data turned in June: payrolls rose just +57K — a clear miss below the ~110K consensus and under the sub-100K "turn" threshold — with a combined −74K of downward revisions and leisure & hospitality shedding −61K. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2%, but only because participation fell. Yet the higher-frequency counter-signal strengthened: initial jobless claims fell to 208K, the fewest in ten weeks and a third straight improvement in the four-week average. Wages ticked up to +3.5% y/y even as hiring cooled — a margin-squeeze mix. Growth itself is holding: June retail-sales control group rose +0.5%, both ISM surveys stayed expansionary (manufacturing 53.3, services 54.0), though industrial production was flat for a second month and the Atlanta Fed's Q2 GDPNow sits at just +1.3% annualised on trade and output drags.

Housing — soft beneath a noisy headline

June housing starts jumped +19% to 1.427M, but the gain was entirely a multi-family rebound; single-family starts fell for a third straight month and permits slipped −3.0% for a second decline. New- and existing-home sales both missed. With the 30-year mortgage stuck near 6.55% and builder confidence (NAHB 34) below 40 for a 15th month, residential activity stays depressed — a continuing drag on GDP's housing component.

Policy — a hold, with the easing door ajar

The Fed (chaired by Kevin Warsh) sits at 3.50–3.75%, its June dot still showing one 2026 cut and its stance explicitly data-dependent. With inflation cooling and the labour leads softening, the 28–29 July meeting looks like a near-certain hold whose real signal is whether Warsh formally opens the September-cut door markets are pricing. Abroad, the ECB (deposit rate 2.25% after six cuts) faces a live July decision as June inflation firmed to +2.7%, and the BoE holds at 4.25% with a 7–2 easing bias. Market-priced conditions corroborate the calm: the Chicago Fed's financial-conditions index is loose (−0.54), the yield curve has dis-inverted (+42bp 10y–2y), high-yield spreads sit near cycle tights at 271bp and the VIX is at 15.7 — no stress signal.
