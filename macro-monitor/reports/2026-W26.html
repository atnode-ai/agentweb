Executive Summary§ 1
The U.S. economy closes June in a sticky-core, firm-growth posture that has hardened the Federal Reserve's hawkish stance. The energy-driven headline spike is peaking — CPI +4.2% y/y, PPI +6.5% y/y — but the Fed's preferred gauge moved the wrong way: core PCE re-accelerated to +3.4% y/y, its highest since early 2024. Growth firmed (Q1 GDP revised up to +2.1%, business capex rebounding), and the FOMC held at 3.50–3.75% with a hawkish bias under new Chair Kevin Warsh, pushing rate relief further out. The labour market is still firm on current data (payrolls +172K) even as its forward indicators drift higher. The pivotal event now is the June jobs report (2 July) — the test of whether the softening labour leads finally bend the firm headline.

What the Data Is Saying§ 3

Inflation — hot headline, and now a stickier core

The energy shock is still inflating top-line prices — CPI +4.2% y/y (energy +23.5% y/y) and PPI +6.5% y/y, the hottest since late 2022 — though with oil rolling over since late May, headline inflation looks to be peaking. The complication came from beneath the surface: while core CPI cooled to +0.2% m/m, the Fed's preferred gauge went the other way — core PCE re-accelerated to +3.4% y/y (from 3.3%), its highest since early 2024. That blunts the "energy-only, contained-core" story and keeps policy tilted toward higher-for-longer.

Labour & growth — firm today, softening at the edges

Hiring is solid on current data: May payrolls beat at +172K, unemployment steady at 4.3%, and wage growth contained (AHE +3.4% y/y, below inflation) — the equity-friendlier "strong jobs without hot wages" mix. But the forward signals keep drifting the wrong way: the smoothed jobless-claims trend hit a fresh 2026 high (4-week average ~224K, continuing claims at a multi-month high), the JOLTS hiring rate slipped to 3.2%, and ISM employment is below 50 in both manufacturing and services. An early-warning alignment — not yet a confirmed turn. Growth, by contrast, is firming: Q1 GDP was revised up to +2.1%, business capex rebounded, and ISM new orders remain strong — so the Fed is not hiking into a visible downturn.

Housing — the forward pipeline is stalling

The leading gauges are now soft in unison: May new-home sales missed badly at 580K (annualised, vs ~640K expected), the second straight weak signing print, while housing starts fell to a five-year low (1.18M) even as permits held. Inventory is piling up (months' supply 10.3) and the 30-yr mortgage sits near 6.47%, so the affordability squeeze persists. Net: cautious-bearish for homebuilders and residential investment.

Policy — globally hawkish, the Fed in a hawkish hold

All three major central banks are now in hold-or-hike mode. The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% at its June meeting — Chair Warsh's debut — but the dot plot moved hawkish and rate relief was pushed further out; futures have priced out near-term cuts. The ECB raised +25bp to a 2.25% deposit rate (its first hike since 2023), hiking into a soft euro-area economy — a textbook stagflationary bind. The BoE delivered a hawkish 7–2 hold at 3.75%, with two members wanting to hike. Sticky services inflation is the common thread.
