What the Data Is Saying§ 3

Inflation — a split story the Fed can't ignore

No new inflation print landed this week, so the split of the last cycle still stands. The energy shock inflates top-line prices — CPI +4.2% y/y (energy +23.5% y/y) and PPI +6.5% y/y on the latest (May) prints — but the core picture has diverged. Consumer core is cooling (core CPI +0.2% m/m, the softest in 2026) and the leading pipeline is easing hard (ISM prices-paid fell across both surveys). Yet the Fed's preferred gauge is moving the wrong way: core PCE +3.4% y/y, the highest since early 2024, fed by services (portfolio management, airfares). That is why the June FOMC minutes (8 July) read hawkish — nine of eighteen policymakers saw a case for a hike by year-end. The swing print is June CPI, now due 14 July (delayed from the date last week's briefing assumed): a cool read hands the Fed room to answer the labour turn; a hot one keeps it boxed in.

Labour & growth — the turn is real, but orderly

The turn confirmed two weeks ago (June payrolls +57K, −74K of revisions, a −61K drop in leisure & hospitality, unemployment's slip to 4.2% mechanical on falling participation) held its character this week rather than deepening. The two escalation tells came back reassuring: ISM services employment rebounded to 51.2 — its first reading above 50 since February, so the weakness has not broadened into services — and weekly jobless claims held at 215K (four-week average 219K), a hiring stall rather than a layoff surge, though continuing claims drifting to 1.81M bear watching. Growth is still expanding, and the consumer looks firmer: both ISM surveys sit comfortably above 50 (manufacturing 53.3, services 54.0) with sharp prices-paid declines in each, and the June retail nowcast (CARTS) points to +1.4% real — a bullish preview for retail sales on 16 July.

Housing — the whole complex is soft in unison

June existing-home sales (9 Jul) completed the picture, missing at 4.09M annualised (−110K versus consensus) even as the median price set an all-time high of $440,600 — an affordability record that caps any near-term recovery. That miss joins a leading pipeline already soft on every leg: May housing starts at a five-year low (1.177M) and May new-home sales at a weak 580K. With the 30-yr mortgage still near 6.43%, the complex is now cautious-bearish for homebuilders and residential-investment GDP. June building permits and starts (~16–18 Jul) are the next read on whether the stall deepens.

Policy & financial conditions — hawkish Fed minutes, calm markets

The Fed holds at 3.50–3.75%, and the June minutes released 8 July leaned hawkish: nine of eighteen policymakers saw a case for a hike by year-end, staff nudged up their 2026–27 inflation projections, and risks were judged tilted to the upside — a committed base case even as the leading data softens. The ECB sits at a 2.25% deposit rate; a soft June HICP flash (+2.7% y/y) opens the door to a cut on 23 July, though a Q2 GDP rebound (+0.3% q/q) tempers the urgency. The BoE stays at 4.25%. Financial conditions remain loose and stable: the yield curve is positive on both slopes (10y−2y +28bp), high-yield spreads sit at a benign 280bp (up 15bp on the week but still below alert), the Chicago Fed's NFCI is well below zero, and the VIX is subdued near 17.6. A broad bond rally (10y −13bp) shows markets leaning toward cuts without forced repricing — no credit stress, which gives the Fed room to answer the labour turn, if core inflation lets it.
