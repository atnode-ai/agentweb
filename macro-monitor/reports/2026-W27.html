What the Data Is Saying§ 3

Inflation — a split story the Fed can't ignore

The energy shock still inflates top-line prices — CPI +4.2% y/y (energy +23.5% y/y) and PPI +6.5% y/y on the latest (May) prints — but the core picture has split. Consumer core is cooling (core CPI +0.2% m/m, the softest in 2026) and the leading pipeline is easing hard (ISM prices-paid fell −9.1pp to 73.0). Yet the Fed's preferred gauge is moving the wrong way: core PCE +3.4% y/y, the highest since early 2024, fed by services (portfolio management, airfares). The swing print is June CPI on 10 July: a cool read hands the Fed room to answer the labour turn; a hot one keeps it boxed in.

Energy lifts the headline figures, but core (ex food & energy) sits well below — the price-shock signature. The wrinkle: core PCE has since crept to +3.4% y/y, the Fed's watch item.

Labour & growth — the turn is now confirmed

June payrolls rose just +57K, a sharp miss versus the ~110K consensus and the first sub-100K "clear-turn" print, alongside −74K of prior-month revisions and a −61K collapse in leisure & hospitality. The unemployment rate's slip to 4.2% was mechanical — participation fell 0.3pp — not genuine tightening, while wages ticked up (AHE +3.5% y/y), a margin-squeezing "softer hiring, stickier pay" mix. Weekly jobless claims remain contained (215K, four-week average 222K), so this is a hiring stall, not yet a layoff surge. Growth is still expanding but moderating: ISM manufacturing 53.3 (a pull-back from May's high) with new orders firm at 56.0 and Q1 GDP revised up to +2.1% — though ISM employment stays below 50.

Housing — the forward pipeline is stalling

The leading gauges are now soft in unison. May new-home sales missed badly at 580K (vs ~640K expected), a second weak signings print, while housing starts sank to a five-year low (1.177M) even as permits held (1.413M) — builders are authorising but not breaking ground, and buyers are not signing. Months' supply has climbed to 10.3 and the 30-yr mortgage sits near 6.47%. Net: cautious-bearish for homebuilders and residential investment. June existing-home sales (9 Jul) are the next read.

Policy & financial conditions — Fed on hold, markets still calm

The Fed holds at 3.50–3.75%; Chair Warsh's debut meeting (16–17 Jun) kept rates unchanged with a hawkish, data-dependent tone, and the June minutes land 9 July. The ECB is easing — a −25bp cut to a 2.25% deposit rate against fragile euro-area growth — while the BoE stays at 4.25% (next decision 7 Aug). Crucially, the swarm's seventh domain — Financial Conditions & Markets — is now live and reads loose and stable: the yield curve is positive on both slopes (10y−2y +28bp), high-yield spreads sit at a benign 280bp, the Chicago Fed's NFCI is well below zero, and the VIX is subdued near 17.6. No credit stress — which gives the Fed room to answer the labour turn, if core inflation lets it.
